# Framing Guide

Use this document when writing player-facing text or issue descriptions about authority, policy, contracts, governors, the board, crises, decrees, or regional conflict.

This is not a prose-quality guide. It is a guide to what is literally happening when the player acts.

Read [setting.md](setting.md) first. This document is the short operational layer on top of it.

## Core Rule

Do not frame the player as a sovereign ruler.

The player is an employee of the N.W.H.O. The N.W.H.O. has formal authority and practical leverage, but not sovereign control. It issues directives, allocates budget, negotiates access, pressures governors, and coordinates response. It does not govern the world by fiat.

Fast check: if a sentence makes the player sound like a president, general, interior minister, or god-camera administrator, it is probably wrong.

## What Player Actions Mean

The UI uses short verbs for clarity. The underlying fiction is usually more specific.

- `Enable Quarantine` means the N.W.H.O. issues a directive and the governor enforces it locally.
- `Build Screening` means the N.W.H.O. funds and stands up a program in cooperation with local authorities and infrastructure owners.
- `Appease Governor` means money, concessions, or compensation are being used to improve compliance.
- `Accept Contract` means the player is accepting a board member's terms, not receiving neutral outside aid.
- `Chairman Approval` is not public legitimacy. It is the chairman's willingness to keep backing you.
- `Funding` is board-controlled operating budget plus attached money from contracts, not tax revenue.

Do not turn correct framing into extra UI ceremony or hidden mechanical complexity.

## Default Verbs

Prefer verbs of coordination, leverage, and compliance.

- `issue`
- `direct`
- `authorize`
- `pressure`
- `negotiate`
- `secure access`
- `buy cooperation`
- `restore compliance`
- `withdraw`
- `reroute`
- `suspend`
- `refuse`
- `accept terms`

Be careful with verbs of direct state power.

- `command`
- `mandate`
- `overrule`
- `seize`
- `deploy troops`
- `federalize`
- `nationalize`
- `appoint`
- `remove`

These are not always forbidden, but they require an actual in-world basis. Most of the time they are the wrong frame.

## Who Cares About What

When writing an event, ask who is actually exerting pressure. If no named actor is applying pressure, the event may be framed too abstractly.

- The board cares about operational continuity, sector damage, regional business exposure, and whether you are protecting their leverage.
- A governor cares about local control, local stability, local patronage, and whether your response threatens their position.
- A corporation cares about contracts, throughput, pricing, infrastructure access, and reputational damage with powerful people, not humanitarian principle.
- The N.W.H.O. cares about keeping the response operational under those constraints.

Do not reach automatically for `the public`, `society`, `the international community`, or unnamed `member states` as the speaker or the audience. In this world, pressure usually arrives through named individuals, board coalitions, governors, lenders, contractors, and infrastructure owners.

## The Most Common Framing Errors

### 1. Fake Sovereignty

Wrong:

- the N.W.H.O. deploys troops
- the player appoints a replacement governor
- the agency overrides local law
- the player issues a federal mandate

Right:

- the N.W.H.O. requests military support
- a successor emerges locally and the player deals with them
- local authorities obstruct enforcement
- the agency issues a directive and pressures for compliance

### 2. NGO / 2020 Institutional Language

Wrong:

- maintain public trust
- combat misinformation
- reassure member states
- preserve institutional legitimacy
- coordinate with the international community

Right:

- stop compliance from collapsing
- contain a leak
- keep a board member on side
- prevent governors from breaking ranks
- secure access, transport, data, or treatment capacity

Words that usually signal the wrong world: `constitutional`, `federal`, `central authority`, `central command`, `national`, `international community`, `public trust`, `global consensus`. Never use `federal` or `central` to describe N.W.H.O. power — the N.W.H.O. issues guidelines and directives, not mandates backed by sovereign authority. Whether any world government exists is deliberately ambiguous.

These are not banned in every context. They are warning lights. Stop and ask whether the line has drifted into 20th-century state or NGO framing.

### 3. Moral Outside-Narration

Wrong:

- the player is trying to save lives but greedy actors interfere
- corporate interests distort the proper response
- politics is corrupting science

Right:

- the board's priorities define the operational response
- a board member wants a term, concession, or policy change
- the player is being evaluated on whether they can keep the machine functioning

The game is not about a pure response being contaminated by outside interests. Those interests are the system.

## Translation Table

Use this as a quick rewrite pass.

- `public trust` -> `compliance`, `credibility with the board`, `local cooperation`
- `misinformation` -> `leak`, `false treatment guidance`, `screening evasion`, `compliance drop`
- `international aid` -> `member-state contribution`, `board-backed assistance`, or `outside funding`, depending on the actual source
- `mandate` -> `directive`, unless the text is specifically about a governor accusing you of overreach
- `civil unrest` -> `perimeter breach`, `staff evacuation`, `rioting`, `looting`, `refusal to comply`
- `government partner` -> `governor`, `regional authority`, `operator`, `board member`, whichever is actually involved
- `economic concerns` -> the specific thing: `shipping losses`, `repriced contracts`, `idle mines`, `grid instability`
- `humanitarian concern` -> the concrete operational concern: `treatment access`, `overflow deaths`, `refugee intake`, `hospital failure`

Prefer the concrete noun over the abstract category.

## Quick Test

Before shipping a line, ask:

- Who is applying pressure here?
- What does the player actually control in this moment?
- Is this describing a directive, a negotiation, a concession, or an enforcement problem?
- Did I accidentally replace a named power relationship with an abstract institution?
- Did I make the player sound morally outside the system instead of inside it?

## Mechanics Versus Description

Do not let correct framing mislead the player about mechanics.

If the player toggles a policy and it takes effect immediately, the text can still say that the governor is enforcing it under pressure. Do not imply long approval pipelines or uncertain execution unless the mechanic actually models that.

If a mechanic really is about partial compliance, defiance, or bargaining, say that plainly. Do not flatten it into clean sovereign control just because the button is simple.

- crisis event descriptions and options
- policy and decree labels
- status messages

## Do Not Use This Document For
