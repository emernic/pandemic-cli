# Setting and Power Structure

This document describes how the game world works. Reference it when writing crisis events, designing features, or framing anything involving relationships between the N.W.H.O., board members, governors, corporations, or regions.

This is not subtext. It is the actual lore of the game. We just don't dump it on the player because that's boring. The player picks it up by playing.

## The N.W.H.O.

The New World Health Organization has a formal global health mandate. On paper, it has authority to set health policy in any region. In practice, that authority is almost never exercised unilaterally.

Think China and Hong Kong. The formal claim of authority is real and everyone acknowledges it. But it only becomes *operational* through relationships, leverage, and the implicit threat of economic consequences. The N.W.H.O. does not roll tanks into regions. It does not override governors by decree. It asks, negotiates, and leans on its board members' economic weight to get things done.

The N.W.H.O. is not a superstate, not a super-UN, not a one-world government. It is an organization that exists because a coalition of powerful people find it useful. The moment it stops being useful to them, it stops existing. The player works for this organization. The player is an employee.

## The Board

The board is the real source of the N.W.H.O.'s authority. Board members are heads of major corporations that control energy, technology, logistics, mining, data infrastructure, and automation globally. Their economic weight is what makes governors listen when the N.W.H.O. talks.

When the player "sets a policy" in a region, what is actually happening is: the N.W.H.O. issues a directive, and the regional governor complies because noncompliance risks economic retaliation from the board members' corporations. A governor who ignores a quarantine order might find their energy contracts repriced, their supply lines rerouted, or their data infrastructure downgraded. This is never stated explicitly in the game. It's just how things work.

The board sets the N.W.H.O.'s operating budget. The player's funding comes from the board, not from regions, not from taxes, not from international aid. If the board is unhappy, funding gets cut. If the board is very unhappy, the player gets fired (game over).

Board members care about their own corporate interests first. A board member whose corporation operates in Asia cares about Asia because that's where they make money, not because they care about the people there. The player who understands this dynamic plays the game correctly.

## Regional Governors

Governors are independent power holders. They have their own authority, their own constituencies, their own agendas. The N.W.H.O. does not appoint governors, does not remove governors, and does not control governors.

Governor cooperation is earned, not commanded. A cooperative governor enforces your policies locally. An uncooperative governor ignores them, undermines them, or actively works against them. The player's tools for dealing with difficult governors are: money (bribes, compensation, economic incentives), board pressure (leveraging a board member's economic presence in the region), and patience.

When a governor dies, the N.W.H.O. does not pick the replacement. A successor emerges through whatever local process exists. The player deals with whoever shows up.

## Corporations

Corporations are the real infrastructure of this world. They run the energy grids, the logistics networks, the data centers, the mines, the factories. Regional economies depend on them. Governors depend on them. The N.W.H.O. depends on them (through the board).

Corporations are not subordinate to the N.W.H.O. They are not partners. They are the entities whose leaders sit on the board and whose economic leverage gives the N.W.H.O. its operational reach. The relationship is: the N.W.H.O. serves at the pleasure of the corporations' leadership, and in exchange gets to coordinate a global health response that (incidentally) protects those corporations' operations and workforce.

## How Policy Enforcement Actually Works

The player toggles a policy (quarantine, border controls, etc.) in a region. What happens next depends on:

1. **Governor cooperation** — A cooperative governor enforces the policy. An uncooperative one doesn't, or does it badly.
2. **Regional stability** — A collapsing region can't enforce anything regardless of cooperation.

## What the N.W.H.O. Cannot Do

- Appoint or remove governors
- Deploy military forces (there is no N.W.H.O. military)
- Override regional authority by fiat (can pressure, but not command)
- Act independently of the board's interests for long
- Compel corporations to do anything

## What the N.W.H.O. Can Do

- Issue health directives that governors usually follow (because of board pressure)
- Allocate its budget to research, personnel, and operations
- Negotiate with governors, corporations, and board members
- Send field teams (with regional cooperation)
- Set up screening, research, and treatment programs
- Accept or refuse contracts from board members

## Using This Document

This is primarily a guide for writing text and framing events. When a crisis event or UI label implies the player has sovereign government powers (deploying military, appointing officials, issuing mandates by fiat), that's the wrong framing. The player's tools are money, personnel, board leverage, and negotiation.

Never use "federal," "central authority," or "central command" to describe N.W.H.O. power. The N.W.H.O. issues guidelines and directives that governors may or may not follow. Whether any world government exists in this setting is deliberately ambiguous.

This does not mean every feature needs layers of intermediaries or hidden modifiers. The game should be clear and playable. The player toggles policies and they take effect. This document is about how we *describe* that process in text, not about adding complexity to the mechanics.
