# Feature Directions

Status: brainstorming only. None of this is implemented. Most of it should be expected to change substantially before any build work starts.

This document exists to preserve promising directions for expanding Pandemic CLI beyond its current reactive outbreak-management core.

## Design Goal

The current game has the beginnings of a workable early game and a potentially strong late game, but the midgame can become passive. The player needs more to do than wait for infection counts to become large enough to matter.

The answer is not to bolt on dozens of tiny overlapping subsystems. The answer is to find a few bold, playable ideas that create strong verbs, strategic identity, and a real sense of building toward something.

## Selection Criteria

Promising directions should usually satisfy most of these:

- create a new source of meaningful decisions in days 10-30
- feel fun and legible as gameplay, not just thematically appropriate
- connect naturally to the existing research / board / regional structure
- give the player a sense of assembling or steering a machine
- transition cleanly into the harsher late-game triage and collapse phase

## Candidate Directions

### 1. Response Network Building

The player gradually assembles a real response apparatus rather than merely toggling policies and waiting.

Core idea:

- build functional links between regions such as sequencing access, trial capacity, manufacturing, logistics, and deployment corridors
- assign regions practical roles inside that network
- lose parts of the network as regions destabilize or collapse

Why it is promising:

- gives the midgame a strong build-up phase
- makes regions matter for what they do, not just their infection counts
- gives late-game collapse something concrete to destroy

Risk:

- could become fiddly infrastructure bookkeeping if it is not reduced to a few strong levers

### 2. Board Members As Concrete Patrons

The board should feel like a coalition of private power centers, not a single approval meter with faces attached.

Core idea:

- each board member has concrete interests, assets, exposed sectors, preferred methods, and demands
- the player manages a shifting coalition rather than a generic abstract mood
- individual patrons can open paths, block paths, or drag the institution toward different styles of play

Why it is promising:

- deepens a system the game already has
- makes contracts, regions, and ledger decisions more legible
- creates a political game that can carry the midgame

Risk:

- could dissolve into noise if every member becomes a constant source of small modifiers instead of a few high-impact conflicts

### 3. Contracts As Strategic Commitments

Contracts should shape the run rather than just add money.

Core idea:

- accepting terms from a patron changes what kind of institution the player is becoming
- contracts create medium-horizon obligations, benefits, and restrictions
- the player can align toward different operating models instead of just taking whichever payout is highest

Why it is promising:

- gives structure to the middle of a run
- turns funding into direction rather than padding
- reinforces the world cleanly without extra lore dumping

Risk:

- if contracts are too mild they remain flavor; if too rigid they become fake choices

### 4. Expanded Field Operations

The player needs more active verbs than policy toggles and research confirmations.

Core idea:

- field teams can secure facilities, recover samples, escort shipments, stabilize clinics, audit data, suppress leaks, restore compliance, or seize material
- personnel becomes a more active strategic resource
- operations create tempo decisions during otherwise quiet periods

Why it is promising:

- fills dead air without waiting for giant outbreaks
- increases agency without needing a full redesign
- can interact with governors, contracts, and crisis chains

Risk:

- could become busywork if missions are repetitive or too obviously correct

### 5. Internal Power Struggle

The agency itself becomes a political battleground.

Core idea:

- deputies, directors, envoys, and security heads build internal factions
- the player appoints, balances, uses, and occasionally purges key figures
- the institution can sabotage itself while outwardly managing the crisis

Why it is promising:

- adds a very different kind of midgame pressure
- makes the N.W.H.O. feel like a dangerous machine rather than a neutral menu shell
- creates memorable storylines and betrayal arcs

Risk:

- a large character layer could pull focus away from the main game if introduced too early

### 6. Human Asset Game

Named people could become part of the player's real toolkit.

Core idea:

- recruit and manage exceptional researchers, operators, fixers, couriers, and political intermediaries
- assign them to departments or missions
- accept that they can burn out, defect, die, or become liabilities

Why it is promising:

- adds attachment and replayable stories
- makes the player care about losses in a more personal way
- can make side content feel more like faction quests than menu maintenance

Risk:

- needs careful scope control to avoid turning into a full character-RPG bolted onto a strategy game

### 7. Counterintelligence / Bio-War Hunt

The outbreak should not have to remain a purely passive disaster.

Core idea:

- investigate anomaly chains, rogue labs, brokers, contaminated supply streams, and engineered lineages
- uncover actors behind outbreaks or opportunists feeding on them
- decide whether to destroy, expose, buy, or repurpose hostile capabilities

Why it is promising:

- creates faction-quest energy and concrete midgame objectives
- fits a world where many threats are engineered or at least suspected to be
- gives the player something to pursue, not just react to

Risk:

- could become generic techno-thriller content if the tone gets too cinematic

### 8. Dual-Use Biological Leverage

This is likely one of the biggest long-term directions for the whole game.

Core idea:

- the same research base that supports countermeasures also enables coercive and offensive capabilities
- the player gradually moves from pure response into design, manipulation, selective protection, suppression, and controlled biological leverage
- this is not a separate "evil mode"; it grows naturally from the same scientific and operational apparatus

Promising forms:

- selective countermeasures that only work for populations the player can access
- biological tools for coercion, containment, sabotage, or denial
- engineered strains or suppressive agents that attack infrastructure, labor pools, food systems, or rival networks
- lineage warfare where one designed biological system interferes with another

Why it is promising:

- creates a major midgame pivot rather than just another panel
- fits the board's likely interests extremely well
- gives the research system a second life beyond purely reactive medicine
- sets up a much stronger bridge into eventual endgame questions about who, if anyone, can still "win"

Risk:

- could become shallow shock content if reduced to simple offensive plague buttons
- needs to stay strategic, operational, and straight-faced

## Strong Current Candidates

If these ideas are narrowed into actual design work, the strongest starting points currently look like:

1. Response Network Building
2. Board Members As Concrete Patrons
3. Contracts As Strategic Commitments
4. Expanded Field Operations
5. Dual-Use Biological Leverage

These seem like the best combination of:

- fun potential
- fit with the world
- compatibility with existing systems
- ability to make the midgame feel active and purposeful

## Likely Shape Of A Better Midgame

A stronger midgame probably involves some version of this arc:

1. Early game: identify threats, stabilize the opening response, learn the current systems
2. Midgame build-up: assemble an apparatus through patrons, contracts, regions, operations, and research choices
3. Midgame pivot: unlock more dangerous or more coercive uses of that apparatus
4. Late game: decide what parts of the apparatus, what regions, and what populations are still worth preserving

That shape is more promising than simply making diseases grow faster or adding more passive modifiers.

## Non-Goals

These notes should not drift into:

- adding many tiny subsystems without a strong play feel
- turning every thematic idea into a separate rule
- confusing complexity with depth
- treating brainstorming documents as implementation specs
