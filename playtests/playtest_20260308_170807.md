Seed: 427236390 | Ticks played: ~525 | Persona: The Economist

## The Hard Truth

The economy in this game is decorative. It looks like it has three resources (Funding, RP, Personnel) creating trade-offs, but in practice there is exactly one binding constraint -- time -- and two of the three resources are irrelevant.

**RP is not a real resource.** I ended the game at tick 525 with 460 RP accumulated and nothing to spend it on. Total RP spent across the entire game: ~55 (two Identifies at 10 each, one Develop at 15, one Manufacture at 10, one more Identify at 10). RP income is +1/tick. RP costs are low and one-time. After the first ~100 ticks, RP is effectively infinite. There is never a moment where I have to choose between two things I want to research because I can't afford both -- I can always afford both, sequentially, with RP to spare. RP is a countdown timer disguised as a resource.

**Funding is barely a constraint.** I started with $1000 and earned +$5/tick. Medicine deployment costs $200 -- a trivial amount. Even with a quarantine policy running at $8/tick (net -$3/tick), I still had $821 at tick 525. I never once had to choose between deploying a medicine and funding a policy. The only scenario where funding could matter is running all three policies on multiple regions simultaneously, but by the time you realize you need that, it's too late anyway.

**Personnel is technically the most interesting resource** because it works differently (pool, not flow). But it never created a real choice either. I have 50 personnel. Research takes 3-5. Quarantine takes 2. I never came close to exhausting my personnel pool. It's a theoretical constraint with no practical bite.

**The medicine dosage economy is the game's deepest problem.** 100K doses per medicine. Manufacturing restores to 100K. A single region can have millions or tens of millions of infected. Deploying 100K doses into 9.6M infected is treating 1% of them. It's not a meaningful intervention -- it's a rounding error. The game gives you a medicine system that literally cannot affect the outcome at the scale the disease operates. By the time you can deploy (tick ~95 at the earliest after identify-identify-develop), South America already has hundreds of thousands infected and growing exponentially. 100K doses is already inadequate at that point, and it only gets worse.

**The result: nothing I did mattered.** I played 525 ticks. I identified both pathogens, developed a medicine, deployed it twice, ran a quarantine. 38.6M people died. My interventions collectively treated maybe 175K people. The disease infected 93.8M. My actions affected perhaps 0.2% of the outcome. The game would have played out almost identically if I had done nothing at all except watch.

## The Experience

I opened the game and immediately liked what I saw: three resources, income rates shown in the header, costs shown on every action. This felt like it was built for someone who thinks about economics. I started planning a resource pipeline: identify at tick 10 (when I have 10 RP), develop at tick 50, deploy by tick 75.

The early game felt promising. I had to wait 10 ticks to accumulate enough RP for my first Identify. That was fine -- the scarcity felt real. I chose to identify Pathogen #1 first because South America (the main outbreak) was the priority. When it completed at tick 30, I learned it was an RNA Virus called Strain Alpha with 6% infectivity. Now I needed either another identify round (to reach 75% knowledge for medicine development) or I could identify the other pathogen. I chose to identify #2 since I could only run one field project at a time and couldn't start bench research yet.

Then came the long wait. Identify #2 finishes at tick 50, but I still need a second identify on Alpha to reach 100% knowledge. That doesn't finish until tick 70. Then Develop Antiviral-A takes 25 ticks, finishing at tick 95. That's 95 ticks of mostly watching numbers go up with very little to do. The pipeline is completely linear and there are no interesting decisions during it -- just "start the next obvious research project."

At tick 95 I had a medicine with 100K doses. South America had 586K infected. I could treat 17% of them. I deployed (untested, which was an interesting risk/reward moment -- the untested warning was genuinely well done). The adverse reaction killed 15K, treated 75K at 75% efficacy (strain had drifted). This was the game's one truly consequential moment: I could see the trade-off between deploying now (untested, outdated strain) versus waiting (people dying while I run clinical trials).

But then I tried to scale up, and the futility hit. Manufacture gives me 100K doses in 15 ticks. Disease is adding millions per tick. I literally cannot catch up. My second deployment of 100K into 9.6M infected North America was completely pointless.

I also tried to activate a quarantine policy on South America around tick 175 but my key sequence failed and it didn't activate. I didn't notice until tick 225 when I checked policies and found "No active policies." This was frustrating -- the game gave no confirmation that a policy was toggled, and the header didn't change, so I had no way to know my action failed until I explicitly checked.

When I finally got quarantine working, the header showed "-$3/t" which was satisfying -- I could see the economic impact immediately. But the quarantine was too little, too late. And the -$3/t burn rate was so slow relative to my $1600+ balance that it posed no real threat.

By tick 525, I was sitting on 460 unused RP, $821 in funds with nothing worth spending on, and 38.6M dead. The game felt over by tick 100 but kept going for another 400+ ticks with nothing to do.

## What I Wanted To Do But Couldn't

- **Spend my RP.** I accumulated 460 RP with nothing to buy. I wanted more research options -- upgrades, infrastructure, scaling dose production, accelerating other projects. Instead the research tree is 5 items deep and then you're done.

- **Scale up medicine production.** 100K doses is laughable against millions of infected. I wanted to invest in manufacturing capacity -- spend $500 to double production, or run multiple manufacture lines. Something that makes the dosage scale with the problem.

- **See my impact.** After deploying medicine, I wanted to see the infection curve bend. It didn't, because 100K doses against millions is invisible. There is no feedback loop connecting spending to outcomes.

- **Make real trade-offs.** I never had to choose between two things I wanted because I could always afford everything eventually. The only constraint was the research slot (one field, one bench), and those just create a queue, not a decision.

- **Deploy partial doses.** Why do I have to deploy all 100K doses? If a region has 5K infected, I'd rather deploy 5K doses and save 95K for another region. The flat deployment model wastes doses.

- **React to the mutation system.** Strain Alpha mutated to Gen 4 by the end. I could see the mutation notifications, but I had no meaningful response. My medicine degraded from 100% to 75% efficacy, and the only fix is to run another clinical trial (15 RP, 25 ticks) -- but efficacy degradation doesn't matter when my doses are already a rounding error against the outbreak.

## Ideas

**The fundamental problem is scale mismatch between player tools and disease growth.** The disease operates in millions. The player operates in hundred-thousands. The game needs either: (a) exponentially scaling player capacity to match disease growth, or (b) disease growth that can actually be contained at the current tool scale.

**Tear out the fixed 100K dose cap, or make it meaningful.** Options:
- Dose manufacturing should scale. First manufacture: 100K. Second: 200K. Third: 500K. Each requires more RP/time/personnel. Now there's a real investment decision: do I manufacture multiple small batches (faster, cheaper) or invest in scaling up production (slower, expensive, but eventually overwhelming)?
- Alternatively, make starting infected counts much lower (hundreds, not tens of thousands) so that 100K doses is a massive intervention. If South America started with 500 infected and grew at 5% per tick, you'd have ~12K at tick 95 -- and 100K doses would be overwhelming. The game would be about early containment, which creates much better decisions.

**RP needs more sinks.** Some options:
- Infrastructure upgrades: +$1/tick income (costs 50 RP + 5 personnel for 30 ticks). Now you're investing RP to improve your economic engine.
- Surveillance networks: spend 20 RP to get early warning of disease spread to new regions (before it appears on the map).
- Dose scaling research: spend 30 RP to double max doses for one medicine permanently.
- Counter-mutation research: spend 25 RP to lock a medicine's strain efficacy at current level for 50 ticks.

**Policy costs should scale with the outbreak.** Quarantine costing a flat $8/tick whether there are 100 infected or 10M infected makes no economic sense. If quarantine cost $8/tick per 100K infected (capped at some max), it would create a genuine trade-off: quarantine early when it's cheap, or wait and pay more later? This mirrors real-world public health economics.

**The funding income system should create visible economic consequences for disease spread.** The design docs mention that deaths reduce regional income, but in practice I couldn't detect this. At 38.6M dead (mostly in South America and North America), my income was still showing +$5/t minus policy costs. If losing South America's population to disease visibly cut my income -- say from $5 to $3 -- that would create a doom spiral where failing to contain the disease makes it harder to contain the disease. That's a real economic feedback loop.

**The deploy decision (vaccinate vs treat) is the game's best mechanic.** It creates a genuine trade-off with no obvious answer. Expand on it: let players choose how many doses to deploy, show the projected impact ("treating 50K of 200K infected will reduce deaths by approximately X over Y ticks"), and make the math hard enough that players agonize.

**Remove or rethink broad-spectrum medicine.** It costs 40 RP / 10 personnel / 50 ticks vs narrow at 15 RP / 5 personnel / 25 ticks. Two narrow medicines cost 30 RP / 10 personnel / 50 ticks (same time and personnel, less RP). There is no scenario where broad-spectrum is the better economic choice. It's a trap option, not a real decision.
