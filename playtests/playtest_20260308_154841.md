## The Experience

This session told a surprisingly compelling story. Two diseases started in Europe (1.4K) and Asia (14.5K). I went through the full pipeline: identify threats, develop medicines, deploy treatments, manage policies, deal with mutations. The narrow vs broad-spectrum trade-off worked well -- I chose narrow medicines for both diseases and the cost/time difference (15 RP/25 ticks vs 40 RP/50 ticks) made that feel like a smart strategic decision rather than a no-brainer.

The high point was around tick 75-155. I had to juggle funding (policies draining $23/tick), research slots (field and bench running in parallel), and decide whether to deploy untested medicines while people were dying. Deploying untested Antibiotic-B to Europe resulted in an adverse reaction that killed 4.6K people -- a genuinely dramatic moment that felt like a consequence of my decision.

The low point was the late game (ticks 200-505). After treating both major outbreaks, diseases persisted at low levels across all regions and slowly grew back. By tick 505, infections had climbed from 51 back to 7.2K. Meanwhile, resources piled up ($2140, 435 RP, 50 personnel) with nothing meaningful to spend them on. The mutation system then kicked in nicely -- by Gen 4, my Antibiotic-B was only 25% effective, creating a clear need to re-trial. But this was the only remaining decision point, and it came after 300 ticks of dead time.

## What Stuck With Me

**The narrow vs broad-spectrum trade-off is real and satisfying.** When I saw Antibiotic-B at 15 RP / 25 ticks vs Broad-Spectrum at 40 RP / 50 ticks, I immediately made a strategic choice. Narrow was the obvious pick for the urgent threat, but I could see how broad-spectrum would be worth it if you were behind on identification. This is a genuine, interesting decision.

**The untested medicine warning is excellent UI.** The panel title changes to include a warning symbol, the text clearly explains the risk (25% chance, 20% of doses kill), and the adverse reaction message at the bottom of the screen after deployment was clear and impactful. When I lost 4.6K people to my own medicine, it felt like *my* fault, not the game being unfair.

**The strain outdated warning is clear and actionable.** At tick 505, trying to deploy Antibiotic-B showed "Strain outdated! (25% strain match -- re-trial to update)" with effective doses dropping from 100K to 25K. This immediately told me what happened and what to do about it. Good feedback loop.

**The early game has genuine tension; the mid-to-late game does not.** The first 150 ticks had real pressure: limited RP, diseases growing fast, hard choices about what to research first. After the initial treatment wave, the game became a waiting room. Nothing bad enough happened to create urgency, but nothing I could do would end the game either.

## What I Wanted To Do But Couldn't

1. **Vaccinate proactively.** I treated infected populations twice, but never vaccinated susceptible ones because the diseases were always small enough to treat. There was no incentive to vaccinate because treatment is reactive and always available. I wanted a reason to vaccinate -- maybe limited medicine supply, or vaccination being much cheaper per dose than treatment.

2. **See a victory or defeat screen.** After 505 ticks, the game was in a limbo state. 7.2K infected, 18.4K dead, diseases slowly growing but not catastrophically. No clear path to victory or defeat. I wanted to know: what are the win/loss conditions? How close am I? The header shows total infected and dead but there is no goal indicator.

3. **Spend my accumulated resources.** By tick 300, I had $2140 and 435 RP with essentially nothing to spend them on. All medicines were developed. No new research to start. Policies were cheap. The economy stopped being a constraint around tick 100 and never became one again.

4. **See diseases broken out per region in the map boxes.** The region boxes show total infected across all diseases, but the threats panel shows per-disease breakdowns. When both diseases were active in a region, I had to open the threats panel to understand which disease was dominant. The selected region's detail view shows disease breakdown, which is great, but only for the selected region.

## Ideas

### 1. Victory/Defeat Conditions with Progress Indicator

The game desperately needs visible win/loss conditions. Without them, it feels like a sandbox with no stakes.

**Concrete proposal:** Add a "Global Threat Level" bar to the header that aggregates infected and dead across all regions. Victory triggers when total infected hits 0 and stays at 0 for some number of ticks (say 50 -- representing sustained eradication). Defeat triggers when total dead exceeds some threshold (say 1% of world population, ~80M) or when all regions reach HIGH threat level simultaneously.

Show this in the header: `Victory: 0/50 ticks at zero infections` or `Threat: 18.4K dead (0.0% of 8B)`. Now the player has something to drive toward, and the late game has tension -- can I eradicate both diseases before mutations outpace my medicines?

### 2. The Late-Game Whack-a-Mole Problem

After the initial treatment wave, the game enters a pattern: diseases grow slowly in all regions, player treats the worst one, diseases grow again. This is tedious because:
- Medicine is always available (no supply limit)
- Resources always exceed costs
- Treatment is instant and total (100K doses vs a few thousand infected)

**What would fix it:** Make medicine supply finite per development cycle. When you develop Antibiotic-B, you get exactly 100K doses. Period. To get more, you need to run another development cycle (or a shorter "manufacturing" research project). Now the player has to think about dose allocation across regions -- do I treat South America's 3.2K infected or vaccinate Europe's 750M susceptible? With only 100K doses, I cannot do both. Suddenly late-game becomes about logistics, not just clicking "treat" whenever numbers get high.

### 3. Resource Sinks for the Late Game

The economy collapses as a meaningful system by tick 100. There is no reason to ever worry about money again.

**Ideas for meaningful money sinks:**
- **Surveillance networks** ($50/tick per region): Reveals real-time R0 and disease growth rate. Without it, you only see current snapshot numbers. This gives the player a reason to spend money on intelligence rather than just treatment.
- **Medicine manufacturing** ($300 + 20 RP, 15 ticks): Produces another batch of 100K doses of an existing medicine. Makes money meaningful throughout the game.
- **Research infrastructure** ($500 one-time): Permanently reduces all research times by 25%. An early-game investment that trades money for time.

### 4. Policy System Feels Disconnected from Victory

Policies (Travel Ban, Quarantine, Hospital Surge) work mechanically but feel disconnected from the moment-to-moment gameplay. I turned on all three policies in Asia, watched numbers go down, then turned them off when the disease was treated. There was no strategic depth to this -- the answer was always "turn on everything in the worst region."

**What would make policies interesting:** Each policy should have a visible downside beyond cost. Travel Bans should reduce funding income from that region (representing economic damage). Quarantine should slow research projects set in that region. Hospital Surge should pull personnel from the global pool, directly competing with research staffing. Now the player faces real trade-offs: "I need to quarantine Asia, but I also need those 2 personnel for my clinical trial."
