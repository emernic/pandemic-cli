# Playtest -- 2026-03-08 13:31

Seed: 7749 | Ticks played: ~121 (victory at tick 121)

## The Experience

I opened the game to a world map showing 6 regions, two already infected: Asia (50K) and South America (500). The game started paused, which was good -- gave me a moment to orient. I unpaused and watched the numbers climb for 20 ticks, then started exploring the research pipeline.

The core gameplay loop was: identify diseases -> develop medicines -> deploy medicines. I identified Strain Alpha first (two rounds of field research, 40 ticks total), developed a Broad-Spectrum Antiviral on the bench (40 ticks), and identified Strain Beta simultaneously with a field project. When the medicine was ready, I deployed it -- untested -- to Asia and South America. Both deployments worked, infections hit zero, and I got a victory screen at tick 121 with 106K dead.

The whole thing felt more like a tutorial than a challenging game. I won comfortably with resources to spare.

## What Stuck With Me

**The untested medicine warning was the best moment.** "25% chance of adverse effects -- 20% of doses will KILL instead of help." That was a genuine decision point. I chose to deploy anyway because Asia was hemorrhaging. I got lucky -- no adverse effects. That moment of uncertainty was the most engaging part of the session.

**The research pipeline is satisfying to understand.** Identify -> Develop -> (optional) Clinical Trial -> Deploy. Each step makes logical sense. Running one field and one bench project simultaneously felt like good resource management.

**The victory screen is well-designed.** Clear summary, per-region breakdown, feels like a proper end state.

**The disease never spread beyond its starting regions.** After 121 ticks, Strain Alpha only existed in Asia and Strain Beta only in South America. No cross-region spread happened at all. This made the game feel very low-stakes -- I only had to worry about two regions. Either the spread probability is extremely low, or the connection lines on the map are decorative.

**The status bar showing research progress is great.** "Field: Identifying Unknown 50%" and "Bench: Broad-Spectrum Antiviral 0%" kept me informed without needing to open panels.

**100K dead felt like nothing.** The victory screen says "0.0% of population" died. With 7.8B total population, even 106K deaths registers as a rounding error. There was never a sense of real threat or urgency.

## What I Wanted To Do But Couldn't

**I wanted to see what happens if I lose.** The game was too easy to find out. There is no loss condition as far as I could tell -- or at least, I would need to deliberately ignore everything for hundreds of ticks to find out.

**I wanted the diseases to spread to new regions.** The whole map felt like decoration since 4 out of 6 regions were never relevant. I wanted to see Europe light up and have to triage where to deploy limited doses.

**I wanted to choose between the two medicines.** Antiviral-A (targets only Strain Alpha) was strictly worse than the Broad-Spectrum Antiviral. No trade-off in cost, capacity, or effectiveness. Why would I ever pick the narrow one?

**I wanted a reason to vaccinate vs. treat.** I treated all infected and won. Vaccination was available but pointless since the diseases never spread. If diseases spread aggressively to new regions, vaccination of uninfected populations would become a real strategic choice.

**I wanted more to do while waiting for research.** After queuing up research, there was nothing to do for 20-40 ticks except watch numbers go up. The Policy panel (placeholder) is where this dead time could be filled.

## Ideas

**Cross-region spread needs to be much more aggressive.** The connection lines between regions are already drawn on the map. Diseases should spread along them within 20-30 ticks, creating cascading crises. Make it feel like a losing race: you identify the disease in Asia, start developing medicine, and meanwhile it pops up in Europe and Africa. Now you have to decide -- do I deploy my limited 500K doses to the region with 200K infected, or split them between three regions that each have 50K? The map connections become the source of strategic tension instead of decoration.

**Medicine trade-offs should create real decisions.** Right now Antiviral-A vs. Broad-Spectrum is a no-brainer. What if narrow medicines were cheaper ($200 vs. $500), faster to develop (20 ticks vs. 40), and more effective (95% cure rate vs. 70%)? Now there is a genuine choice: do I rush the narrow medicine and save Asia right now, or invest in the broad-spectrum that will cover multiple threats but takes longer and costs more? The narrow medicine becomes the "stopgap" and the broad-spectrum becomes the "endgame solution."

**Difficulty scaling through disease mutation or new outbreaks.** After you contain the first wave, a mutated strain could appear that the existing medicine does not cover. Or a completely new disease emerges in a previously clean region. This turns the late game from "coast to victory" into "manage cascading crises with depleted resources."

**The Policy panel could be the economic decision layer.** Ideas: travel restrictions between regions (slow spread but reduce funding income from trade), quarantine zones (reduce infection rate but cost personnel), public communication campaigns (reduce panic but cost funding), emergency funding requests (get a big cash injection but with a cooldown). Each policy should have a meaningful downside so it is not a free power-up. For example: "Close borders between Asia and Europe. Reduces spread rate by 80% on that route but cuts your funding income by $2/tick. Do you accept the long-term economic hit to buy time?"

**Event system for narrative moments.** "Strain Alpha has mutated -- lethality increased by 50%." "A vaccine shipment was contaminated -- you lose 100K doses." "Scientists in Europe volunteer to assist -- +10 personnel for 30 ticks." These would break up the "wait for research bar to fill" monotony and force reactive decision-making.

**Personnel should feel more constrained.** With 50 personnel and projects costing 5-10, I never felt the pinch. If starting personnel were 20, every research project would be a bigger commitment, and the player would feel the trade-off between running multiple projects and having enough people to staff field hospitals or manage deployments.
